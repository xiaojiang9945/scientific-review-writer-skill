# Plant Kinetochores as Gated Interfaces Between Centromere Identity, Checkpoint Control, and Chromosome Engineering

**Abstract**

Plant kinetochores translate centromere chromatin into microtubule attachment, tension sensing, and chromosome transmission. The field has moved from cataloguing centromeric repeats and marker proteins toward a gated view of kinetochore function: CENH3 and CENP-C define an inner platform, centromeric transcripts and rapidly evolving repeats tune the probability of productive assembly, KNL2 and histone chaperones control CENH3 loading, the KMN network creates the microtubule-binding interface, and spindle assembly checkpoint proteins convert attachment state into cell-cycle decisions. This review synthesizes direct evidence from *Arabidopsis thaliana*, maize, rice, barley, rye-related materials, and holocentric plants, with model-system comparisons used only where they clarify conserved logic. The central tension is that plant centromere DNA evolves quickly, whereas chromosome segregation requires a heritable and mechanically reliable kinetochore. This tension explains both the robustness of natural centromeres and the fragility of engineered systems. CENH3-mediated haploid induction, synthetic maize centromeres, KNL2 manipulation, and de novo centromere formation show that plant kinetochores can be rewired, but their deployment is constrained by centromere strength, cell-cycle timing, parental epigenetic mismatch, environmental sensitivity, and cross-generation stability. A quantitative framework for kinetochore strength is now the main bottleneck for connecting plant cell biology to crop chromosome engineering.

**Keywords:** plant kinetochore; centromere; CENH3; CENP-C; KNL2; KMN network; spindle assembly checkpoint; haploid induction; chromosome engineering

## Introduction

Plant kinetochores sit at a narrow but consequential interface: they must preserve chromosome identity across mitotic and meiotic divisions, yet remain responsive enough to correct attachment errors and to reset during reproduction. A centromere can be described cytologically as a primary constriction, genomically as a repeat-rich or structurally distinct chromosomal domain, epigenetically as CENH3-containing chromatin, and functionally as the region that builds a kinetochore competent for microtubule attachment. These definitions overlap, but they are not identical. The distinction matters in plants because centromere DNA varies rapidly among species, because many crops have large and repeat-rich genomes, and because some plant lineages have holocentric chromosomes in which spindle attachment is distributed rather than concentrated at a single primary constriction. Recent reviews of plant kinetochores and centromeres have therefore shifted the field from a static definition of centromere position to a functional question: which molecular gates decide whether a chromosomal region becomes a heritable, force-bearing kinetochore [1-3]?

This review uses "kinetochore" in a strict functional sense. A plant kinetochore is not simply a CENH3 signal, nor is it only a microtubule-binding patch. It is a multilayer protein-nucleic-acid assembly that reads centromere chromatin, recruits an outer attachment interface, monitors incorrect attachments, and changes its behavior in meiosis, embryogenesis, and engineered chromosome systems. This layered definition is useful because many important plant cases violate a simpler one-to-one model. CENH3-rich chromatin may not always predict the full region of spindle attachment in holocentric systems. Repeats can be abundant at a centromere but not sufficient to create a functional kinetochore. A weakened centromere can still segregate in one context but be eliminated after fertilization. Conversely, a chromosome fragment can acquire a de novo centromere if chromatin and cell-cycle conditions favor productive assembly.

The thesis of this review is that plant kinetochores are best understood as gated interfaces. The first gate is centromere identity: CENH3, CENP-C, centromeric RNA, satellite repeats, retrotransposons, and local chromatin states create a probabilistic platform for kinetochore assembly. The second gate is CENH3 loading and maintenance: KNL2, histone chaperones, post-translational modification, and proteolysis restrict where and when centromeric nucleosomes are deposited. The third gate is outer-kinetochore assembly: NDC80, MIS12-associated proteins, KNL1, and related factors convert inner identity into a microtubule-coupled interface. The fourth gate is surveillance and reset: spindle assembly checkpoint proteins, phosphatases, chromosomal passenger components, cohesin, and meiotic regulators decide whether attachment is acceptable, delayed, corrected, or deliberately altered.

The evidence base is uneven but increasingly strong. *Arabidopsis thaliana* provides genetics, cell-cycle timing, embryo studies, and recent checkpoint work. Maize provides large-kinetochore cytology, CENH3/CENP-C/NDC80 evidence, centromere RNA, artificial chromosome systems, and haploid-induction engineering. Rice and barley reveal centromere organization in large crop genomes and show that centromere function cannot be inferred from a single repeat family. Holocentric plants such as *Luzula* and *Cuscuta* test whether CENH3 distribution, spindle attachment, and chromosome geometry can be decoupled. Crop engineering studies then ask whether these mechanisms can be controlled well enough to produce useful haploids, synthetic chromosomes, or stable introgression materials.

This synthesis is also timely because long-read genome assemblies and improved cytological markers are changing what can be asked. Earlier plant centromere studies often had to infer function from repeat enrichment, immunostaining in a few tissues, or segregation phenotypes after large chromosomal rearrangements. Current work can combine chromosome-scale assemblies, CENH3 or CENP-C profiling, protein localization, mutant genetics, and transmission assays. That combination exposes a recurring mismatch: the genomic region that looks like a centromere, the chromatin region that carries CENH3, and the region that recruits a microtubule-coupled outer kinetochore may be overlapping but not perfectly equivalent. The mismatch is not a technical nuisance. It is the biological space in which centromere repositioning, genome elimination, and synthetic centromere formation become possible.

The sections below move from measurement boundaries to mechanism, then to species diversity and engineering. This order is intentional. Kinetochore biology becomes confusing when marker proteins, DNA repeats, chromosome behavior, and breeding applications are discussed as if they were equivalent readouts. A full synthesis needs to specify what each measurement actually supports. Immunolocalization of CENH3 supports centromere identity, but not necessarily checkpoint competence. KNL1 or NDC80 localization supports functional outer-kinetochore assembly more directly. Haploid induction demonstrates a transmission bias, but not by itself which molecular defect caused genome elimination. Separating these readouts makes it possible to interpret plant kinetochore studies as a coherent mechanism rather than as a list of proteins and unusual chromosome behaviors.

## Measurement Architecture: What Counts As A Functional Plant Kinetochore?

The first bottleneck in plant kinetochore research is not a missing protein name, but a measurement problem. Centromere identity, kinetochore assembly, microtubule attachment, chromosome movement, checkpoint signaling, and inheritance stability are often measured with different assays. A CENH3 ChIP-seq peak, a CENP-C immunostaining signal, an NDC80 focus, a metaphase alignment defect, and a haploid-induction phenotype are all informative, but they do not report the same layer of the system. A high-level review must keep these readouts separated because the strongest conclusions come from studies that combine them.

Inner-centromere markers define the most widely used entry point. CENH3 is the plant centromeric histone H3 variant, and its centromeric localization in *A. thaliana* helped establish an epigenetic view of plant centromere identity [4]. Maize provided an important crop example by showing that CENH3 interacts with CentC satellite repeats and centromeric retroelements, thereby linking CENH3 chromatin to specific repeat environments without reducing centromere identity to those sequences [5]. CENP-C adds a second inner-kinetochore layer. In maize, a CENP-C homolog is a constitutive inner kinetochore component [6]. Together, CENH3 and CENP-C define an inner platform, but a platform is not yet a complete attachment machine.

Centromere-encoded RNA and CENP-C binding refine this boundary. Maize centromere-encoded RNAs were found as integral components of the kinetochore [7], and CENP-C DNA binding can be stabilized by single-stranded RNA [8]. These findings support a model in which centromeric transcription and RNA-protein interactions tune inner-platform stability. They also explain why a repeat-rich region should not automatically be treated as a functional centromere. If RNA state, CENH3 deposition, and CENP-C binding are not aligned, repetitive DNA may remain only a sequence feature rather than a kinetochore-forming domain.

Outer-kinetochore markers are closer to function. NDC80 is the best-known microtubule-binding interface in many eukaryotes, and maize NDC80 is a constitutive feature of the central kinetochore [9]. Arabidopsis SPC24 and NUF2 homolog studies show that NDC80-complex components affect cell division, spindle organization, chromosome segregation, and development [10-11]. More recent work argues that KNL1 and NDC80 are useful universal markers for detecting functional centromeres in plants [12]. This distinction is important for difficult systems: a CENH3 signal can mark centromeric chromatin, whereas KNL1/NDC80 signals better indicate that an outer kinetochore has assembled.

Checkpoint readouts define yet another layer. A plant cell can assemble kinetochore proteins but still fail to delay anaphase after incorrect attachment. Conversely, prolonged checkpoint signaling can indicate unresolved attachment even if centromere identity is intact. Recent work in Arabidopsis and maize has connected KNL1, BMF3/BUB3.3, CDC20, and phosphatase recruitment to spindle assembly checkpoint signaling and silencing [13-17]. These studies move plant kinetochore biology toward functional circuitry: the key question becomes how a plant kinetochore switches from "wait" to "go" once microtubule occupancy and tension reach acceptable thresholds.

The assay hierarchy should therefore be explicit. CENH3 ChIP or immunostaining is strongest for locating centromeric chromatin. CENP-C strengthens the inference that the inner kinetochore is assembled. KNL1, NDC80, NUF2, and SPC24-family markers are stronger evidence for an outer kinetochore. Live-cell or staged cytology of alignment, lagging chromosomes, and anaphase bridges reports the mechanical output of the attachment layer. Checkpoint protein localization or delayed anaphase reports surveillance. Genetic transmission, haploid induction, and multigeneration stability report the final inheritance outcome. When a paper combines only one or two of these layers, the claim should remain layer-specific. For example, a CENH3-rich neocentromere candidate is not yet a fully validated functional kinetochore until outer markers and segregation behavior are also shown.

This hierarchy is particularly important in large crop genomes, where repeats can mislead both assembly and annotation. A tandem repeat family enriched near the primary constriction may be a useful landmark, but it may not define the active CENH3 domain. Conversely, active genes or low-copy sequences inside a centromere do not exclude kinetochore function, as rice centromere studies have shown [18]. For crop genomics, the safest operational definition of a functional centromere is a convergent one: chromosome-scale sequence context, CENH3 and CENP-C occupancy, outer-kinetochore marker recruitment, and stable transmission. The more engineered or rearranged the chromosome, the more this convergence matters.

Inheritance stability is the final and most stringent readout. A kinetochore that functions in one mitosis may still be unstable over meiosis, fertilization, embryo development, or multiple generations. This issue is central to plant chromosome engineering. CENH3-based haploid inducers, artificial maize centromeres, and de novo centromeres are successful only when altered kinetochore behavior produces a predictable transmission outcome rather than random chromosome loss. For this reason, functional plant kinetochore studies should ideally connect four measurements: inner identity, outer assembly, checkpoint behavior, and inheritance. Very few systems currently satisfy all four, which is why evidence-ranked wording remains necessary.

## Centromere Identity And CENH3 Loading Are Controlled By Multiple Gates

The strongest conceptual shift in plant centromere research is that centromere DNA is neither irrelevant nor sufficient. Plant centromeres frequently contain satellite repeats and centromere-targeted retrotransposons, but those elements vary rapidly and can be absent from stable functional chromosomes. Barley chromosomes without typical centromeric repeats can still be stable [19], and rice centromere sequencing revealed active genes within a functional centromere [18]. These observations do not mean that sequence has no role. Rather, sequence creates a local chromatin environment that can favor or disfavor CENH3 nucleosome maintenance, CENP-C binding, transcription, and recombination suppression. Function emerges from the interaction between sequence and epigenetic state.

This interaction is visible at several scales. At the nucleosome scale, CENH3 replaces canonical H3 in centromeric chromatin and provides a heritable mark that is read by inner kinetochore proteins. At the domain scale, satellite repeats and retrotransposons can concentrate CENH3-compatible chromatin and help maintain a repeat-rich centromeric environment. At the chromosome scale, long arrays of repeats, structural variants, and DNA methylation shape recombination landscapes and centromere-proximal inheritance. T2T-era Arabidopsis centromere work shows cycles of satellite and transposon evolution [20], while structural variation and DNA methylation influence centromere-proximal meiotic crossover frequency [21]. Thus, the centromere is a dynamic domain that protects segregation while also shaping genetic exchange.

The rapid evolution of centromeric DNA creates a paradox. Segregation requires a stable cellular address, yet the sequence content of that address changes across species and even among accessions. One resolution is that plant cells inherit a chromatin state rather than a precise sequence string. Repeats and retrotransposons can support that state by nucleating local chromatin environments, but they are replaceable over evolutionary time. This view also explains why centromeric repeats can expand or contract without immediately destroying chromosome transmission. The kinetochore reads a chromatin and protein context that is biased by sequence, not a universally conserved DNA motif.

The first mechanistic gate is CENH3 deposition. In *A. thaliana*, CENH3 loading occurs mainly during G2 and requires the histone fold domain [22]. During meiosis, CENH3 loading has a distinct reproductive timing [23]. These direct plant data constrain any engineering model that treats CENH3 abundance as a simple input. CENH3 must be available at the right cell-cycle stage, incorporated into the correct chromatin context, and retained across transitions when chromosomes are reorganized. A centromere that is adequate in mitotic tissues may behave differently in male or female meiosis, after fertilization, or in early embryogenesis.

KNL2 provides a second gate upstream of CENH3. Arabidopsis KNL2 is required for normal CENH3 deposition at centromeres [24], and its centromere targeting depends on a conserved CENPC-k motif in the C terminus [25]. Plant KNL2 has also undergone recurrent plant-specific duplications while retaining a conserved function as a kinetochore assembly factor [26]. This pattern is informative: plants appear to preserve the function of a CENH3-loading gate while allowing lineage-specific variation in the proteins that implement it. KNL2 therefore sits at a useful intersection between conserved centromere biology and plant-specific regulatory evolution.

Histone chaperones and protein turnover sharpen the gate. NASP contributes to de novo deposition of CENH3 in Arabidopsis early embryogenesis [27]. APC/C-CDC20-driven KNL2 proteolysis is critical for centromere integrity and mitotic fidelity [28]. These studies show that centromere identity is controlled not only by recruitment but also by removal, timing, and dosage. In a gated model, too little CENH3 weakens kinetochore identity, but poorly restricted CENH3 loading could create ectopic assembly potential or blur centromere boundaries. Protein turnover protects the system by preventing assembly factors from persisting outside their productive window.

Centromeric transcription adds another layer of control. The maize RNA studies indicate that centromere-derived transcripts are part of the kinetochore environment [7-8]. Rice and Arabidopsis centromere analyses further support the idea that transcription and epigenetic state are not accidental background features of centromeres [18,20-21]. Recent rice work reporting augmented CENH3 loading with transcriptional and epigenetic reprogramming during meiosis strengthens this view in a crop genome [29]. These data point to a model in which centromeric transcription can either reinforce kinetochore identity or expose centromere-proximal regions to regulatory change, depending on timing and chromatin context.

The gate is likely tissue-specific. Mitotic meristems, meiocytes, gametophytes, and early embryos impose different demands on centromere chromatin. Meristematic divisions require repeated faithful segregation. Meiosis requires monopolar orientation in the first division and altered centromere cohesion. Early embryos may expose parental asymmetries because paternal and maternal centromeres have just entered the same cytoplasm. A CENH3 or KNL2 variant that appears functional in vegetative tissues may therefore fail under reproductive timing. This is one reason why haploid-induction phenotypes can be difficult to predict from steady-state protein localization alone.

One implication is that plant centromere identity should be measured as a state, not as a single marker. A weak or transitional state may show only some of these features. This distinction can explain why genome elimination, neocentromere formation, and artificial centromere transmission are threshold phenomena. They depend not on whether CENH3 is present, but on whether the combined state is strong enough to recruit a functional outer kinetochore and compete with other centromeres in the same cell.

## The Outer Kinetochore Converts Identity Into Force, Surveillance, And Release

Once centromere identity is established, the outer kinetochore must convert it into a microtubule-coupled interface. In plant cells this task occurs without canonical animal centrosomes, within a cell-wall-constrained geometry, and often within large or polyploid genomes. The conserved KMN network provides the core logic: the NDC80 complex binds microtubules, MIS12-associated proteins connect layers, and KNL1 coordinates checkpoint signaling. Plant studies now support the presence and functional importance of these layers, even though sequence divergence and plant-specific organization can make homology difficult to recognize.

Maize NDC80 localization established that a conserved microtubule-binding component is present at plant kinetochores [9]. Arabidopsis MUN, encoding an SPC24 homolog of the NDC80 complex, affects development through cell division [10], and AtNUF2 modulates spindle microtubule organization and chromosome segregation during mitosis [11]. These phenotypes demonstrate that NDC80-complex proteins are not redundant markers. They are needed to organize microtubule attachment and therefore influence plant development. The proposal that KNL1 and NDC80 serve as universal plant functional-centromere markers follows from this logic [12]: they report the assembly of the attachment layer more directly than inner chromatin marks alone.

The conversion from identity to force is a structural and regulatory transition. Inner centromere proteins must recruit outer components in a geometry that allows microtubule plus ends to bind, release, and rebind until chromosomes achieve the correct orientation. In plants, this transition is complicated by acentrosomal spindle organization and the need to coordinate chromosome movement with a phragmoplast-based cytokinesis program. Although these broader cell-division features are not unique to the kinetochore, they set the mechanical context in which plant kinetochores operate. A defect in NDC80-complex assembly can therefore appear as a spindle-organization, chromosome-alignment, or developmental phenotype, rather than as a purely centromeric defect.

KNL1 extends the outer kinetochore from structure to signaling. A functional framework in Arabidopsis places kinetochore proteins and spindle assembly checkpoint components in a coordinated pathway [13]. In maize, KNL1 participates in spindle assembly checkpoint signaling [14]. In Arabidopsis, a coadapted KNL1 and checkpoint axis supports precise mitosis [15]. These findings are important because they show that plant KNL1 is not merely a passive tether. It helps determine how attachment errors are sensed and how the cell delays anaphase until chromosomes are properly attached.

Checkpoint signaling in plants has recently gained molecular resolution. The Arabidopsis BUB1/MAD3 family protein BMF3 requires BUB3.3 to recruit CDC20 to kinetochores during spindle assembly checkpoint signaling [16]. After correct attachment, Arabidopsis KNL1 recruits type one protein phosphatase to kinetochores to silence the checkpoint [17]. These studies provide both sides of the switch: one module helps create a wait signal, and another helps terminate that signal. The result is a plant-specific implementation of a broadly conserved principle. Kinetochore attachment is not accepted simply because microtubules are nearby; it must satisfy a biochemical checkpoint circuit that can be turned off only after attachment state and tension are adequate.

Error correction also depends on phosphorylation-centered modules. The plant chromosomal passenger complex has been functionally analyzed in relation to chromosome segregation [30], and Haspin-mediated histone H3 phosphorylation regulates chromosome alignment and segregation in maize [31]. These data support a tension-sensitive regulatory layer in which kinase and phosphatase activities tune kinetochore-microtubule attachments. The exact spatial architecture may differ from animals, but the logic is similar: incorrect or low-tension attachments remain unstable and checkpoint-active, whereas correct bipolar attachments are stabilized and checkpoint-silenced.

This signaling architecture creates a useful conceptual distinction between attachment occupancy and attachment quality. A kinetochore can be occupied by microtubules but still lack the correct tension or geometry. In that state, the checkpoint and correction machinery should remain active. A mature bipolar attachment must both resist force and silence the checkpoint. This distinction is essential for interpreting mutants with partial chromosome alignment, delayed anaphase, or mild aneuploidy. Such phenotypes may not indicate failure to assemble a kinetochore; they may indicate failure to tune attachment quality or to silence the checkpoint at the right time.

The outer-kinetochore gate is especially relevant under stress. Heat stress impairs centromere structure and meiotic chromosome segregation in Arabidopsis [32]. This evidence suggests that reproductive heat sensitivity can involve centromere and kinetochore instability, not only pollen metabolism or floral development. In crops, high temperature could affect CENH3 retention, KNL2 activity, microtubule dynamics, checkpoint timing, or meiotic orientation. These possibilities remain under-tested, but they identify a practical research direction: meiotic segregation stability should become part of climate-resilience phenotyping for crops with known reproductive heat sensitivity.

The current limitation is quantitative. Plant studies often report signal presence, signal loss, or visible chromosome missegregation. What is missing is a calibrated scale for kinetochore strength. Such a scale would combine CENH3/CENP-C abundance, KNL1/NDC80 recruitment, attachment lifetime, checkpoint duration, segregation error rate, and inheritance across generations. Without this scale, it is difficult to compare natural centromeres, weakened CENH3 variants, synthetic centromeres, and introgressed chromosomes. With it, plant kinetochore biology could move from descriptive cytology to predictive chromosome engineering.

## Meiosis, Holocentricity, And Crop Genomes Reveal The Range Of Plant Kinetochore Solutions

Meiosis changes the operating rules of the kinetochore. During mitosis, sister kinetochores normally attach to opposite spindle poles. During meiosis I, sister kinetochores must act as a unit so that homologs, not sisters, segregate. Cohesin and meiotic chromosome architecture therefore become part of kinetochore function. In Arabidopsis, AtREC8 and AtSCC3 are essential for monopolar orientation of kinetochores during meiosis [33]. In rice, OsREC8 is required for chromatid cohesion and metaphase I monopolar orientation [34]. These direct plant studies show that meiotic kinetochore behavior is not a passive consequence of centromere identity. It is actively coordinated with cohesion, pairing, and recombination.

This meiotic view reframes centromere-proximal recombination. Centromeres are often treated as recombination-poor regions, but reduced recombination is an output of chromatin, structure, cohesion, and kinetochore environment rather than a fixed property of DNA. Arabidopsis structural variation and DNA methylation shape centromere-proximal crossover landscapes [21]. If kinetochore assembly and centromere chromatin also change during meiosis, as suggested by rice CENH3 loading and epigenetic reprogramming [29], then centromere-proximal recombination should be viewed as part of a broader meiotic centromere state. This matters for crop genetics because low recombination around centromeres can trap deleterious alleles, limit introgression, and complicate map-based selection.

Holocentric plants provide a natural stress test for definitions. In *Luzula elegans*, meiotic chromatid segregation follows an alternative pattern consistent with holocentric chromosome behavior [35]. In *Cuscuta europaea*, mitotic spindle attachment to holocentric chromosomes does not simply correlate with the distribution of CENH3 chromatin [36]. More recent work on *Luzula sylvatica* shows repeat-based holocentromeres and provides insight into transitions toward holocentricity [37]. These systems demonstrate that CENH3 distribution, chromosomal geometry, and microtubule attachment can be reorganized in plants. They also caution against using a monocentric crop model as the universal reference for all plant kinetochores.

Crop genomes add a different kind of diversity. Maize has large centromeres with abundant repeats and a rich history of cytogenetic tools. Rice provides sequenced centromeres with active genes and tractable genetics. Barley and wheat-related materials reveal how centromere function persists in large, repeat-rich, rearranged, or introgressed chromosomes. In these systems, kinetochore biology is not an abstract cell-biological topic. It determines whether alien chromosome arms transmit, whether engineered fragments persist, whether B chromosomes behave selfishly, and whether centromere-proximal regions can be recombined or selected effectively.

This crop diversity also changes the interpretation of "conserved" components. A conserved protein name can mask different quantitative requirements in different genomes. A maize kinetochore assembling over a large repeat-rich domain, a rice centromere containing active genes, and an Arabidopsis centromere embedded in a smaller genome may all use CENH3, CENP-C, KNL1, and NDC80, but the spatial scale, repeat context, and tolerance for partial weakening may differ. Polyploid crops add another layer because homoeologous chromosomes, alien introgressions, and structural rearrangements can place centromeres into non-native regulatory environments. The key question becomes not whether the protein toolkit is conserved, but how robustly that toolkit is read in each chromosomal context.

The practical lesson is that plant kinetochore mechanisms must be interpreted in species context. Arabidopsis is powerful for genetics and checkpoint mechanisms, but its small genome and centromere structure do not capture all crop constraints. Maize is strong for cytology and artificial centromere work, but conclusions about maize repeat arrays may not transfer directly to rice or wheat. Holocentric systems reveal alternative chromosome solutions, but they should not be treated as direct models for monocentric cereals. A disciplined evidence hierarchy therefore helps: use direct evidence for the species or chromosome system when available, use close crop comparisons when necessary, and use general eukaryotic models only to clarify conserved principles.

For breeding and germplasm work, this hierarchy should be built into material evaluation. A line carrying an alien chromosome segment should not be judged only by agronomic phenotype or molecular markers flanking the segment. If the material includes a centromere, centromere-proximal rearrangement, telocentric chromosome, or minichromosome, functional kinetochore markers can reveal whether transmission risk is likely to emerge later. The same logic applies to artificial chromosomes. A construct that is visible cytologically and transmitted in early generations still needs testing for kinetochore-marker stability, meiotic behavior, and environmental sensitivity.

## Engineering Plant Kinetochores: From Genome Elimination To Synthetic Centromeres

The most visible application of plant kinetochore biology is haploid induction. In Arabidopsis, altered CENH3 can produce haploid plants through centromere-mediated genome elimination [38]. A CENH3 point mutation can impair centromeric CENH3 loading and induce haploid plants [39]. These results show that weakening or mismatching centromere identity can bias chromosome transmission after fertilization. The mechanism is not simply loss of CENH3. It is a competition between parental centromeres and kinetochores during early embryo divisions, where weaker or epigenetically mismatched centromeres are more likely to be eliminated.

Several studies sharpen this threshold model. High temperature increases centromere-mediated genome elimination frequency and enhances haploid induction in Arabidopsis [40], indicating that environmental conditions can shift the stability of weak centromeres. Epigenetically mismatched parental centromeres can trigger genome elimination in hybrids [41], showing that relative centromere state between parents matters. These findings explain why haploid induction efficiency varies across genotypes and conditions. A useful inducer must be weak enough to be eliminated in the desired context, but not so unstable that it causes broad sterility, severe developmental defects, or unpredictable aneuploidy.

Engineering has now extended beyond CENH3 variants alone. Synthetic maize centromeres can transmit chromosomes across generations [42]. De novo centromere formation on a maize chromosome fragment demonstrates that new functional centromeres can arise in plant chromosomes under suitable conditions [43]. More recently, the CENH3 assembly factor ZmKNL2 was reported to boost haploid induction in maize [44]. These studies show that plant kinetochore engineering can target not only the centromeric histone itself but also assembly factors and chromosomal substrates. The engineering target is therefore the centromere state, not a single protein.

The distinction between an inducer and a stable engineered chromosome is important. Haploid induction often benefits from a controlled weakness or mismatch, because the desired outcome is elimination of one parental genome. Synthetic centromeres and minichromosomes require the opposite: they must recruit enough kinetochore activity to transmit reliably. De novo centromere formation sits between these outcomes because it reveals how a new chromosomal region can cross the threshold into stable function. These three applications use the same biological machinery but select for different positions on the strength spectrum.

At the same time, cautionary evidence is essential. Rice OsCENH3 mutant lines generated for haploid induction caused chromosome instability and aneuploidy but did not efficiently trigger haploid formation [45]. This result is valuable precisely because it is not a simple success story. It shows that CENH3 engineering is species- and context-dependent. A strategy that works in Arabidopsis or improves maize induction cannot be assumed to transfer directly to rice. Differences in CENH3 structure, compensatory loading, embryo development, genetic background, and selection against unstable gametes may all shift the outcome.

For breeding, the central engineering metric should be kinetochore strength. Too much strength preserves chromosomes that should be eliminated. Too little strength causes random loss, sterility, or aneuploidy. A useful engineering window must be narrow: it should produce predictable elimination or transmission without destabilizing the rest of the genome. This window also depends on developmental stage. A centromere weakened in pollen may not behave the same way after fertilization. A synthetic centromere stable in controlled generations may still need testing under heat, field environments, and different genetic backgrounds.

The most useful engineering pipeline would therefore include staged go/no-go thresholds. The first threshold is molecular: does the altered centromere recruit CENH3, CENP-C, and outer markers in the intended cells? The second is cytological: does it align and segregate without excessive lagging chromosomes or bridges? The third is developmental: does it preserve fertility or produce the intended haploid or elimination phenotype? The fourth is breeding-level: does it remain stable across genetic backgrounds and environments? A design that passes the first threshold but fails the third is a mechanistic tool, not a deployable breeding system.

The next generation of plant kinetochore engineering should therefore integrate cell biology with breeding pipelines. Candidate CENH3, KNL2, or centromeric-repeat designs should be evaluated with CENH3/CENP-C/KNL1/NDC80 localization, live or fixed-cell segregation assays, embryo-stage chromosome tracking, haploid or aneuploid counts, and multigeneration transmission tests. Engineering should also include negative metrics: sterility, off-target chromosome loss, abnormal recombination, and environment-sensitive failure. This broader validation would prevent a narrow focus on induction rate from hiding the chromosome instability that limits deployment.

## Outlook

Plant kinetochore biology now has enough molecular detail to support a predictive framework, but not yet enough quantitative integration to make that framework routine. The field can identify CENH3, CENP-C, KNL2, NDC80, KNL1, and checkpoint components in many contexts. It can show that centromeric repeats and transcripts influence kinetochore identity, that CENH3 loading is cell-cycle regulated, and that altered centromeres can trigger genome elimination. The remaining challenge is to measure how these components combine into a functional threshold.

Three bottlenecks define the next phase. The measurement bottleneck is the absence of a cross-species kinetochore-strength scale. A practical scale would integrate inner-marker intensity, outer-marker recruitment, checkpoint duration, microtubule attachment stability, segregation error frequency, and inheritance across generations. The mechanism bottleneck is the incomplete definition of plant inner-kinetochore architecture beyond CENH3, CENP-C, KNL2, and RNA-associated features. The translation bottleneck is that engineered centromere behavior remains sensitive to species, genotype, developmental timing, and environment.

The most useful experiments will connect these bottlenecks rather than address them separately. For example, CENH3 or KNL2 variants should be tested not only for haploid induction but also for quantitative CENP-C, KNL1, and NDC80 recruitment, checkpoint timing, and early embryo chromosome behavior. Heat-stress experiments should measure centromere chromatin and kinetochore markers directly, rather than scoring only fertility or meiotic abnormalities. Crop introgression and artificial-chromosome projects should include functional kinetochore markers, not only repeat annotation and cytological presence.

There is also a data-integration bottleneck. Plant centromere studies increasingly produce long-read assemblies, ChIP-seq, methylomes, RNA data, immunostaining, mutant phenotypes, and transmission ratios, but these data are often reported in parallel rather than combined into a single functional model. A practical next step would be a plant kinetochore evidence matrix that records, for each chromosome or engineered construct, whether inner identity, outer assembly, checkpoint response, meiotic behavior, and inheritance stability have been demonstrated. Such a matrix would make evidence gaps visible and would prevent overinterpretation of any single marker.

The field should also treat negative engineering outcomes as mechanistic information. A failed haploid inducer, an unstable synthetic centromere, or a heat-sensitive meiotic centromere can identify the edge of the functional window. Reporting these cases with the same molecular detail as successful systems would make it possible to distinguish weak CENH3 loading, poor outer-kinetochore recruitment, defective checkpoint silencing, and developmental selection against unstable chromosomes. That distinction is essential if kinetochore engineering is to become predictable rather than empirical.

The larger implication is that the plant kinetochore is both a safeguard and a tool. It protects inheritance by buffering rapidly changing centromere DNA, but that same epigenetic flexibility allows genome elimination, de novo centromere formation, and synthetic chromosome transmission. The field will progress fastest by treating robustness and engineerability as two outcomes of the same gated system.

## Outstanding Questions

- What quantitative combination of CENH3, CENP-C, KNL1, NDC80, checkpoint duration, and segregation error rate best defines plant kinetochore strength?
- Which plant proteins or RNA-mediated interactions substitute for the animal inner-kinetochore architecture that is difficult to identify by simple sequence homology?
- How do centromeric transcripts, R-loops, satellite repeats, and retrotransposons control CENP-C binding and CENH3 nucleosome stability?
- Why do CENH3- or KNL2-based haploid-induction strategies transfer efficiently in some plant systems but fail or produce aneuploidy in others?
- How does heat stress alter CENH3 loading, KMN recruitment, checkpoint signaling, and meiotic chromosome orientation in crop reproductive tissues?
- Can de novo or synthetic plant centromeres be designed with predictable strength rather than selected empirically after chromosome fragmentation?
- Which marker combination best predicts the stability of alien chromosomes, B chromosomes, minichromosomes, and engineered fragments in breeding programs?
- How many generations and environments are required before an engineered plant centromere can be considered deployment-stable?
