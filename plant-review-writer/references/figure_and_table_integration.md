# Figure And Table Integration

## Principle

Display items must do scientific work. They should clarify a mechanism, boundary, decision framework, evidence hierarchy, species comparison, or workflow that the text needs.

## Common Figure Roles

- Boundary figure: separates related concepts and defines the problem.
- Architecture figure: links environment, phenotype, mechanism, and outcome.
- Mechanism figure: shows causal gates, tissues, compartments, or regulatory modules.
- Comparative figure: shows species, crop, lineage, or system differences.
- Pipeline figure: connects measurement, omics, genetics, modeling, validation, and deployment.

## Section-Derived Figure Workflow

1. Write or revise the section first.
2. Identify the conceptual question the figure answers.
3. Extract required biological elements, labels, arrows, gates, feedbacks, and contrasts from the section.
4. State what the figure must avoid because the manuscript does not support it.
5. Generate or revise the figure.
6. Place the caption below the image.
7. Cite the figure in the text where the reader needs it.

## Visual Quality

- Use readable labels.
- Keep line weights consistent.
- Use white or very pale backgrounds unless the target design requires otherwise.
- Prefer restrained scientific color palettes.
- Avoid decorative gradients, unrelated icons, thick arrows, stray panel letters, auto-added legends, and manuscript-style titles inside the image.
- Match plant morphology to the topic.

## Tables

Use tables only when columns make a decision or synthesis clearer. Good columns include evidence class, species, tissue, assay, causal layer, validation status, deployment status, and bottleneck.
